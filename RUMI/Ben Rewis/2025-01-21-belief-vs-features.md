# Belief vs Features Framework

From Ben Rewis, Jan 21, 2025

## Key Insight

Belief is what gets investors, customers, and teammates to care; features are just how the belief is delivered today. The more your startup sells a clear, emotionally resonant story about a better future and your unique way of getting there, the less you get trapped competing on checklists and incremental app features.

## Belief vs Features Hierarchy

- **Feature**: What your product does (e.g., "1-click sharing," "AI scoring")
- **Benefit**: What users get from that (e.g., "saves you 3 hours a week")
- **Belief**: What they must accept for the benefit to matter (e.g., "time is my scarcest asset; I should automate anything repeatable")

If you only talk features, you invite comparison with every competitor; if you lead with belief, you define the game people think they're playing.

## Core Beliefs You Need to Sell

For an early-stage startup, the key beliefs usually cluster around:

1. **The problem**: "This problem is urgent, expensive, and overlooked by current solutions."
2. **The future**: "There is a different, better way to live/work if we change our approach."
3. **The category**: "The old category is broken; this new category is the right lens."
4. **The team**: "This founding team is uniquely qualified to make that future real."

Features then become proof points that your worldview is executable, not the main event.

## How to Build and Communicate Belief

### Craft a Vivid Product Vision Story

- Tell a concrete story of a future day-in-the-life with your product in it, showing how a specific person's world is transformed, not which buttons they tap
- Highlight the emotional payoff (relief, confidence, status, freedom) alongside functional outcomes (time saved, revenue gained)

### Articulate a Sharp Point of View (POV)

- Name what's broken in the old way and why it will get worse if people stay there
- Coin language for the new way: a named problem, a named practice, even a named category, so people can repeat your story when you're not in the room

### Use Your Pitch as "The Product"

- Treat your deck, landing page, and narrative as the first product users experience; they must change how people think before they ever log in
- Make every demo start with context and stakes ("Here's what's happening in your world and why it will crush you if you don't adapt") before clicking through UI

## Avoiding the "Feature Factory" Trap

- Don't treat every customer request as a roadmap item; map requests against your vision and core beliefs, and say no to anything that dilutes them
- Anchor metrics and success stories in outcomes (NPS, retention, revenue lift, time saved) rather than raw feature count or velocity
- Use "strong opinions, loosely held": have clear beliefs about the model and category, then update them with real customer evidence instead of chasing a laundry list
